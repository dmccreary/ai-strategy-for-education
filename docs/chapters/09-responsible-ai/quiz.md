# Chapter 9 Quiz — Responsible AI

Test your understanding of the ethical considerations, legal requirements, and risk management practices that make AI deployment in education safe and trustworthy. Questions cover Remember, Understand, Apply, and Analyze levels of learning.

## Questions

??? question "1. What is Responsible AI, and what are its core principles as they apply to education?"
    **Answer:** Responsible AI is the practice of designing, deploying, and governing AI systems in ways that are safe, fair, transparent, and accountable — minimizing harm and maximizing benefit for all users. In education, core principles include fairness (ensuring the AI does not disadvantage students based on race, gender, disability, or socioeconomic status), transparency (students and families can understand how the AI works and what data it uses), privacy protection (student data is handled lawfully), and human oversight (educators retain meaningful control over consequential decisions).

??? question "2. What is Algorithmic Bias, and how can it emerge in educational AI systems?"
    **Answer:** Algorithmic Bias is a systematic error in an AI system's outputs that produces unfair outcomes for certain groups — such as consistently underestimating the ability of students from particular racial, gender, or socioeconomic backgrounds. In educational AI it can emerge when training data reflects historical inequities (e.g., grading patterns that penalized certain dialects), when proxy variables correlated with protected characteristics are inadvertently included in models, or when the populations used to develop and test the AI are not representative of the students who will actually use it. Regular equity audits are essential to detect and correct bias.

??? question "3. What is FERPA, and what does it require of schools when deploying AI tools that access student data?"
    **Answer:** FERPA — the Family Educational Rights and Privacy Act — is a federal law that gives parents (and students over 18) the right to access their education records and restricts schools from disclosing those records without consent. When deploying AI tools, FERPA requires schools to ensure that vendors only receive the minimum student data necessary for the educational purpose, that vendor contracts include data security and use restrictions, and that families can access and correct records. Schools must conduct due diligence to verify that AI vendors are FERPA-compliant before granting them access to student data.

??? question "4. What is COPPA, and why is it particularly relevant for AI tools used by elementary students?"
    **Answer:** COPPA — the Children's Online Privacy Protection Act — requires operators of websites and online services directed at children under 13 to obtain verifiable parental consent before collecting personal information from those children. It is particularly relevant for elementary AI tools because younger students are less able to understand or protect their own privacy, and the potential harms from misuse of their data are especially serious. Schools deploying AI tools for K-5 students must verify that vendors comply with COPPA and that parents have been informed about what data is collected.

??? question "5. What is the risk of AI Hallucination in an educational context, and how should teachers address it with students?"
    **Answer:** In an educational context, AI Hallucination — where an AI generates confident-sounding but factually incorrect or fabricated information — poses the risk that students accept wrong information as fact, damaging the quality of their learning and any work they produce. Teachers should address it by explicitly teaching students that AI tools can be wrong, modeling the habit of verifying AI-generated claims against authoritative sources, and framing AI as a drafting or brainstorming tool rather than a final authority. Building source verification into assignment requirements is an effective structural approach.

??? question "6. What is Over-Reliance on AI, and what student skills are most at risk from it?"
    **Answer:** Over-Reliance on AI occurs when students (or educators) habitually defer to AI tools for tasks they should be developing the capacity to perform themselves, resulting in the atrophy of foundational skills. Student skills most at risk include writing (if AI always produces the first draft), mathematical reasoning (if AI always solves problems without explanation), critical evaluation of information (if AI always summarizes sources), and research skills (if AI always determines what information is relevant). The goal is to use AI as a scaffold that is gradually removed as skills develop, not as a permanent substitute for student effort.

??? question "7. What is Skill Atrophy in the context of AI use, and why is it a long-term concern for students?"
    **Answer:** Skill Atrophy is the gradual weakening of a capability due to lack of practice — analogous to a muscle weakening from disuse. In an AI-rich environment, students who rely on AI for spelling, calculations, writing, and navigation may fail to develop or maintain those competencies. The long-term concern is that these foundational skills often underpin higher-order thinking and are needed in contexts where AI is unavailable or unreliable. Schools must deliberately design learning experiences that require students to practice core skills independently, even when AI tools are available.

??? question "8. What is Academic Integrity in the context of AI, and how should schools update their policies to address it?"
    **Answer:** Academic Integrity means that student-submitted work honestly represents the student's own learning and effort. AI challenges traditional academic integrity because AI-generated text can pass as student writing, making it hard to know whether a submission reflects the student's understanding. Schools should update policies by clearly defining which AI uses are permitted (e.g., brainstorming, grammar checking) versus prohibited (e.g., submitting AI-written text as one's own), teaching students why integrity matters rather than just enforcing rules, and redesigning assessments to include elements — such as verbal defense, process documentation, or in-class writing — that are difficult to fake.

??? question "9. What are AI Detection Tools, and what are their limitations in educational settings?"
    **Answer:** AI Detection Tools are software applications that attempt to identify whether a piece of text was generated by an AI rather than a human, based on statistical patterns in the writing. Their limitations in educational settings include high false-positive rates (flagging student writing as AI-generated when it is not), high false-negative rates (missing AI-generated text that has been lightly edited), the ease with which AI text can be modified to evade detection, and the disproportionate impact on students who are non-native English speakers (whose writing patterns may superficially resemble AI text). Relying solely on detection tools for academic integrity enforcement is unreliable and potentially unfair.

??? question "10. What is the principle of Transparency in AI, and what does it mean practically for school administrators?"
    **Answer:** Transparency in AI means that the operation, decision-making logic, and data use of AI systems are open to scrutiny by affected stakeholders rather than hidden inside opaque systems. For school administrators it means: communicating clearly to families what AI tools are in use and what data they access; requiring vendors to explain how their AI makes recommendations; providing students with explanations of AI-generated feedback; and publishing the school's AI use policies openly. Transparency builds community trust and enables meaningful oversight, which is a legal and ethical requirement rather than merely a best practice.

??? question "11. What is Explainability in AI, and how does it differ from Transparency?"
    **Answer:** Explainability refers to the ability to explain, in terms humans can understand, why an AI system made a specific decision or produced a specific output — for example, 'the early alert system flagged this student because her quiz completion rate dropped 40% this week.' Transparency is broader — it means the overall system's purposes, data use, and policies are open. Explainability is focused on individual outputs. Both matter in education: Transparency builds general trust, while Explainability is necessary for teachers and parents to take appropriate action on AI recommendations without having to blindly accept or reject them.

??? question "12. What is Human-in-the-Loop design in AI systems, and why is it especially important for consequential educational decisions?"
    **Answer:** Human-in-the-Loop design means that AI systems are designed to keep human judgment at the center of significant decisions rather than making those decisions fully autonomously. In education, consequential decisions include special education placement, disciplinary action, course advancement, and graduation requirements — decisions that significantly affect a student's life. Requiring human review before these decisions are finalized ensures that errors, biases, or gaps in AI judgment are caught before causing harm. AI should inform these decisions, but humans with professional accountability should make them.

??? question "13. What is Misinformation Risk in AI-assisted education, and how does it differ from simple AI hallucination?"
    **Answer:** Misinformation Risk is the broader concern that AI tools used in education may present contested, misleading, or ideologically skewed content as factual — not just through accidental hallucination but through systematic biases in training data or deliberate manipulation. It differs from hallucination in that misinformation may be factually plausible but subtly distorted — for example, presenting a one-sided view of a historical event. Schools should address this by teaching media and AI literacy as core curriculum, curating AI tools that are built on vetted content, and requiring students to compare multiple sources rather than accepting any single AI response.

??? question "14. What is AI Safety as a concept, and how does it translate to practical considerations in K-12 school deployments?"
    **Answer:** AI Safety in its broad research sense concerns the challenge of ensuring that highly capable AI systems behave in alignment with human values and do not cause unintended harm. In K-12 deployments, it translates to practical considerations such as ensuring AI tutors do not provide harmful advice to vulnerable students, that AI content filters work reliably to block inappropriate material, that AI systems do not manipulate student emotions to increase engagement in harmful ways, and that there are clear escalation protocols when AI detects a student in distress. Age-appropriate safeguards and regular safety audits are essential.

??? question "15. What is Student Data Protection, and what practical steps should a district take before deploying any AI tool that accesses student information?"
    **Answer:** Student Data Protection encompasses all the legal, technical, and policy measures that ensure student information is collected only for legitimate educational purposes, stored securely, shared only with authorized parties, and deleted when no longer needed. Before deploying any AI tool accessing student data, a district should: review the vendor's data processing agreement for FERPA and COPPA compliance; conduct a data privacy impact assessment; confirm that student data will not be used to train or improve the vendor's AI models; establish breach notification procedures; and notify families about what data is collected and how it is used. These steps protect students and reduce the district's legal liability.
